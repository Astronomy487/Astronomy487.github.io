Voldo

Voldo is a personal engelang based on predicate logic, unambiguity, a degree of simplicity and consistency, and personal preferences. This language is still a work in progress. Special thanks to Vrabbers for the auto TOC and help troubleshooting.

Phono

Phonology

Voldo has 6 vowels and 10 consonants. Consonants are built on three places of articulation and do not have voicing distinctions. Characters on the IPA tables are in the typical Roman orthography.

It is preffered that consonants are pronounced voiced, but voiceless consonants would of course be interpreted the same.

All syllables are either CV or CCV. The only disallowed clusters are repeated consonants.

Orthography

The Voldo Roman orthography contains 16 letters: a b d e g i l m n o r u v z ŋ ə. All these letters are pronounced the same as their IPA equivalents, with the exception of r, the velar fricative.

Text in Voldo can be written in all lowercase and without punctuation. If a writer or reader decides to, however, it can be written with capitalization and punctuation for any reason.

The Predicate

Predicates

A predicate connects a set of arguments and defines their relationship. The predicate can be thought of as the verb of a clause, and the arguments can be thought of as the nouns of a clause, although they are subtly different.

All predicates, as they appear in the dictionary, are at least 2 consonants of the form C(C)aC(C)a, where each C is any consonant and each a is a dummy vowel. The vowels of a predicate are changed to inflect the predicate. An example of a predicate is:

rana: speaker¹ says message² to audience³ through medium⁴

Predicates can be loaned into Voldo for any use, especially proper nouns.

Loanwords

A loanword, to be identified as a loanword, must contain at least 3 syllables. The first two syllables are inflected like any other predicate, and any extra vowels from additional syllables retain the vowel in their root (meaning zadaro and zadari are separate predicates). The most common use for borrowing a word into Voldo is proper nouns. Their positions take the formats listed in the section on predicate groups.

Predicate Groups

Predicate groups are groups of predicates with similar meanings or argument slot structures. In the predicate list, predicates belonging to groups are marked with a tag like this: UNIT. Functionally, predicates within a group behave identically to predicates outside the group. Predicate groups are not limited to predicates from the base vocabulary, as NAME and REGION are also predicate groups. This list contains all predicate groups and their argument slot structures:

  • UNIT unit¹ measures amount² of quantity³
    • unit¹ is the item being measured, which defaults to a measurement of one unit if no amount² is provided
  • NAME name¹ is called or named name by group² in context³
    • name¹ is an or the entity that goes by the name
  • REGION region¹ is the geographic region of region, inhabited by inhabitants²
    • region¹ is the region that goes by the name
  • LANGUAGE language¹ is the language of language spoken among speakers² to express content³ through medium⁴
    • region¹ is the region that goes by the name

The names of these argument slots change for each predicate. For example, the second slot of any predicate in the REGION group is never inhabitants²; it is always pertaining to the specific predicate, like Wisconsinites² in the case of the predicate corresponding to Wisconsin.

These groups all have a predicate that contains a generic structure, one of which every other predicate in the group satisfies; these are the words for unit, name, etc. It is from these predicates that the form for the group is made. These predicates are also tagged as part of the group.

The Argument

Arguments

Predicates can support anywhere from 0 to 4 arguments. These come in slots, and the use of a slot is defined by the predicate. For example, in the clause I speak to you, I is the speaker in position 1, and you is the audience in position 2. All of these arguments, even if they seem unrelated, are analyzed as different players of what is fundamentally one action or state.

Further, arguments can be derived from predicates. The word speaker, defined as something that satisfies position 1 of rana, can be used as an argument for any new predicate, like position 2 of balna. The word form of the argument is a form of rana from position 1 to position 2, which is roni. Altering the vowels allows for 16 derivations of a predicate to be used as an argument.

Arguments can also be formed without predicates by pronouns and nominal clauses.

Pronouns

Pronouns are a type of argument not derived from a predicate. The simplest pronoun is the generic pronoun (lo¹, li², le³, lu⁴). On its own, it makes no more sense to include it than to not include it, but it is combined with pronoun modifiers to indicate additional meaning.

Nominal Clauses

A nominal clause encloses a clause or clauses and treats them as an argument for another clause. This is also how quotations are indicated. The position of the nominal clause determines which particle opens it (zo¹, zi², ze³, zu⁴), and the particle zə closes a nominal clause.

Modifiers

Numbers

Numbers are used to quantify an argument and, in rarer occasions, a predicate.

The simpler way to express positive integers is to list digits from left to right using corresponding particle words (ve 1, ŋo 2, vo 3, və 4, ŋə 5, do 6, de 7, ŋe 8, də 9, na 0). Each particle is its own word and is not concatenated for numbers (də bu lə 487). Negative integers are the same as their positive counterparts but with bla before them.

All non-integers are represented with the form m×10^n, where m is a number between 1 (inclusive) and 10 (exclusive) and n is any integer. Non-integers are expressed by stating m using digits (it is inferred the decimal point comes after the first digit), the particle zdo, and n (an integer). The negative particle bla can come before m and n. This system can also be used for integers to round numbers and shorten text.

12.5663706 → 1.256×10^1 → 1 2 5 6 zdo 1 → ve ŋo ŋə do zdo ve

Two extra number particles, znu and zni, multiply or divide quantities by 1000 respectively. ŋo znu ŋo na, literally 2 ×1000 2 0, means 2020.

A number always quantifies the argument or predicate that comes before it (four teachers, loŋo də). This number is also how singular is specified (one teacher, loŋo ve). When rə, the negation conjunction for arguments, is used in conjunction with a singular, it is implied to mean plural (teachers, loŋo ve rə).

These number particles need spaces between them to prevent them from being interpreted as a predicate.

Also classified as number particles are particles for operations (gli +, blu -, ŋli ×, mlu ÷, bva ^, dza √, gra log, gze (, dzo )).

Numbers are considered a predicate modifier, just like compounds, meaning they can be strung after a predicate or argument and extend the meaning (a number can be put before or after the modifier of a compound and be valid).

Compounds

Compound predicates are formed when several predicates are combined. This is also how adjectives and adverbs are formed. In a compound predicate, only the first predicate is inflected, and all the following predicates that modify it take a new compound inflection. This inflection is determined by the position of the modifier (o-ə¹, i-ə², e-ə³, u-ə⁴) (not necessarily the position of the argument as a whole).

Compounds are also used for all derived units, quantifying things like energy, velocity, force, and more. The particles vŋo ^2, vŋə ^3, and vŋe ^4 are used for these derived units. A watt (unit of power) is defined as kg⋅m²⋅s⁻³, expressed as gram ×1000 meter ^2 ÷ second ^3, producing molgo znu govlə vŋo mlu roŋgə vŋə. Most of these derived units have their own predicates, in this case ŋanda.

Relative Clauses

A relative clause describes an argument of a clause and provides another clause it satisfies. In the clause I see the person who has a dog, the main clause is I see the person, and the person is further described as satisfying position 1 of [they] have a dog. The position satisfied in the relative clause is indicated by the particle that opens the relative clause (no¹, ni², ne³, nu⁴). Hence, the [they] does not have to be at all stated in the relative clause. There is also a separate particle, nə, to close the relative clause. Relative clauses can be embedded within other relative clauses.

Relative clauses are also a predicate modifier, like numbers, although they can only be used on arguments.

Connectives

Conjunctions

Conjunctions connect two clauses or two arguments and says something about their truth. It cannot connect two predicates with the same set of arguments because those arguments will take different roles in a new predicate.

There are five base types of conjunction: three commutatives (and ∧, xor ⊕, or ∨), two non-commutatives (if then →), and one that takes a single argument (not ¬).
