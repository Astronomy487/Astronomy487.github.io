Voldo is a personal language based on predicate logic.

Inspirations for Voldo include Toaq, Toki Pona, and Lojban.

Phonology

Voldo has 6 vowels and 10 consonants. Consonants are built on three places of articulation and do not have voicing distinctions. Characters on the IPA tables are in the typical Roman orthography.

Diphthongs are rarely but sometimes used, and all 36 vowel combinations are possible.

Orthography

The Voldo Roman orthography contains 16 letters: a b d e g i l m n o r u v z ġ ȧ.

Text in Voldo can be written in all lowercase and without punctuation. If a writer or reader decides to, however, it can be written with capitalization and punctuation for any reason.

Predicates

A predicate connects a set of arguments and defines their relationship. The predicate can be thought of as the verb of a clause, and the arguments can be thought of as the nouns of a clause, although they are subtly different.

All predicates, as they appear in the dictionary, are at least 2 consonants of the form C(C)aC(C)a, where each C is any consonant and each a is a dummy vowel. Typical predicates are 3 consonants, common predicates are 2 consonants, and 4-consonant predicates are unused in the base vocabulary. These vowels are changed to inflect the predicate. An example of a predicate is:

rana: speaker¹ says message² to audience³ through medium⁴

Predicates can be loaned into Voldo for any use, especially proper nouns.

Arguments

Predicates can support anywhere from 0 to 4 arguments. These come in slots, and the use of a slot is defined by the predicate. For example, in the clause I speak to you, I is the speaker in position 1, and you is the audience in position 2. All of these arguments, even if they seem unrelated, are analyzed as different players of what is fundamentally one action or state.

Further, arguments can be derived from predicates. The word speaker, defined as something that satisfies position 1 of rana, can be used as an argument for any new predicate, like position 2 of balna. The word form of the argument is a form of rana from position 1 to position 2, which is roni. Altering the vowels allows for 16 derivations of a predicate to be used as an argument.

Compound Predicates

Compound predicates are formed when several predicates are combined for use as a predicate or argument. This is also how adjectives and adverbs are formed. In a compound predicate, only the first predicate is inflected, and all the following predicates that modify it take a new compound inflection. This inflection is determined by the position of the modifier (o-ȧ¹, i-ȧ², e-ȧ³, u-ȧ⁴).

Loanwords

A loanword, to be identified as a loanword, must contain at least 4 consonants or at least 3 syllables. To meet four consonants, it should take the form of CCvCCv and take vowel inflections like any other predicate. If a loanword has at least 3 syllables, the first two syllables are inflected like any other predicate, and any extra vowels from additional syllables retain the vowel in their root (meaning zadaro and zadari are seperate predicates).

The most common use for borrowing a word into Voldo is proper nouns. Their positions take this format:

zadaro: Zadaro¹ is called or named Zadaro by group² in context³

Here, something that satisfies the first position is Zadaro themself, something that satisfies position 2 is those who call them Zadaro, and something that satisfies position 3 is the context where they're called Zadaro.

Relative Clauses

A relative clause describes on argument of a clause and provides another clause it satisfies. In the clause I see the person who has a dog, the main clause is I see the person, and the person is further described as satisfying position 1 of [they] have a dog. The position satisfied in the relative clause is indicated by the particle that opens the relative clause (no¹, ni², ne³, nu⁴). Hence, the [they] does not have to be at all stated in the relative clause. There's also a separate particle, nȧ, to close the relative clause. Relative clauses can be embedded within other relative clauses.

Conjunctions

Conjunctions connect two clauses or two arguments and says something about their truth. It cannot connect two predicates with the same set of arguments because those arguments will take different roles in a new predicate.

There are five base types of conjunction: three commutatives (and ∧, xor ⊕, or ∨), one non-commutative (if then →), and one that takes a single argument (not ¬). Nominal Clauses

A nominal clause encloses a clause or clauses and treats them as an argument for another clause. This is also how quotations are indicated. The position of the nominal clause determines which particle opens it (zo¹, zi², ze³, zu⁴), and the particle zȧ closes a nominal clause.
