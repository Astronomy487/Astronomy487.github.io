Concept

Jȧrgo is a personal language based on predicate logic.

Phonology

Jȧrgo has 6 vowels and 10 consonants. The vowels come in three pairs, each differing from their partner by vowel depth. Consonants are built on four places of articulation and do not have voicing distinctions.

Predicates

A predicate connects a set of arguments and defines their relationship. The predicate can be thought of as the verb of a clause, and the arguments can be thought of as the nouns of a clause, although they are subtly different.

All predicates, as they appear in the dictionary, end in -na, -ne, or -ni. From this base form, a total of 20 forms can be created by altering the consonant and the vowel depth. These 20 forms are used to convey grammatical information. Wordlists define predicates like this:

rane: speaker¹ says message² to audience³ through medium⁴

Arguments

Predicates can support anywhere from 0 to 4 arguments. These come in slots, and the use of a slot is defined by the predicate. For example, in the clause I speak to you, I is the speaker in position 1, and you is the audience in position 2. All of these arguments, even if they seem unrelated, are analyzed as different players of what is fundamentally one action or state.

Further, arguments can be derived from predicates. The word speaker, defined as something that satisfies position 1 of rane, can be used as an argument for any new predicate, like position 2 of jadroni. The word form of the argument is a form of rane from position 1 to position 2. Of the 20 predicate forms, 16 are used to form arguments from predicates. This argument derivation process also includes the alteration of vowel depth between the three vowel pairs. This chart shows the 16 derivations using the vowel pair i-u.

Compound Predicates

Compound predicates are formed when several predicates are combined for use as a predicate or argument. This is also how adjectives and adverbs are formed.
